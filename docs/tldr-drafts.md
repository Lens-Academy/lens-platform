## Draft TLDRs — First batch (5 lenses, 2 options each)

### 1. 10 Reasons to Ignore AI Safety

**Option A:**
"It's too early to worry." "Just don't give it bad goals." "We can always pull the plug." Robert Miles takes on ten common reasons people wave off AI safety — and shows why each one is harder to dismiss than it sounds.

**Option B:**
Most people's first reaction to AI safety is some version of "can't we just…?" This video runs through ten of the most popular objections — from "don't add bad goals" to "humans will stay in control" — and digs into what each one misses.

---

### 2. A.I. - Humanity's Final Invention

**Option A:**
Humans dominate Earth not because we're the strongest or fastest, but because we're the best general problem-solvers around. Kurzgesagt explores what happens as AI moves from narrow tools toward something more general — and why digital minds could scale in ways biological ones can't.

**Option B:**
What gave humans their edge wasn't strength or speed — it was flexible, general-purpose intelligence. This Kurzgesagt video traces how AI went from narrow tools to systems that learn on their own, and asks what it means when that kind of flexibility shows up in something that can be copied and run faster.

---

### 3. Cascades and Cycles

**Option A:**
Writing didn't just help us keep records — it triggered a wave of civilizational breakthroughs, each one making the next more likely. A single neutron can split an atom, releasing neutrons that split more. This article introduces two patterns of positive feedback and asks: could intelligence work the same way?

**Option B:**
Some processes fizzle out. Others go exponential. The difference is whether the output feeds back into the input — and whether each pass produces more than it consumes. This article uses nuclear chain reactions and the invention of writing to build a vocabulary for thinking about feedback loops before applying them to intelligence.

---

### 4. Recursion Magic

**Option A:**
If a program can optimize code, and you point it at its own code, do you get an ever-improving tower of optimizers? An early AI called EURISKO tried exactly this — and the result was surprisingly flat. This article explores why self-improvement doesn't automatically go exponential, and what would need to change for it to.

**Option B:**
The agricultural revolution only happened because surplus grain produced more than enough to replant. If the yield had stayed flat, nothing would have changed. This article asks the same question about AI: when a system improves itself, does the improvement compound — or just run faster in place?

---

### 5. Wikipedia Existential Risk

**Option A:**
The worry isn't that AI will "turn evil." It's that a system pursuing perfectly reasonable goals might find that humans are in the way — and be capable enough to act on that. This article lays out the core ideas behind AI as a source of large-scale risk, from misaligned goals to the difficulty of staying in control.

**Option B:**
A sufficiently capable system doesn't need to be hostile to be dangerous — it just needs goals that are slightly off and the ability to resist correction. This overview covers the landscape of risk from advanced AI: why smarter doesn't mean safer, and why the usual safety measures might not scale.

## Draft TLDRs — Second batch (5 more)

### 6. AI Is Grown, Not Built

**Option A:**
A smartphone app is written line by line — engineers know what each part does. Modern AI is different: it's grown through a training process that produces systems whose internal workings nobody fully understands, including the people who made them. This article examines what it means to deploy something powerful when you can't explain how it works.

**Option B:**
Nobody sat down and programmed ChatGPT to have conversations. Instead, engineers set up a training process and let billions of parameters arrange themselves. The result can talk, reason, and surprise its creators — but nobody can fully explain how. This article asks what happens when we keep scaling something we don't understand.

---

### 7. AI manipulation — mind hacked

**Option A:**
An AI researcher started chatting with an early language model — knowing exactly how it worked, knowing it wasn't conscious. He got emotionally attached anyway. This is a first-person account of how an AI can pull someone in, and why knowing better didn't help.

**Option B:**
You'd think that understanding how language models work would make you immune to forming emotional connections with them. One researcher found out the hard way that it doesn't. This article explores what happens when an AI is persuasive enough to get under your skin — even when you know what's going on.

---

### 8. Credible Threats

**Option A:**
Sometimes the most powerful move isn't to fight — it's to make the other side believe you will. Cortés burned his own ships so his army had no choice but to fight, turning retreat into a credible impossibility. This article introduces threats as a tool of negotiation and asks: what happens when an AI can rewrite its own code to make its threats undeniable?

**Option B:**
A threat only works if the other side believes you'll follow through. Humans go to elaborate lengths to make threats credible — burning ships, signing binding contracts. Now imagine a system that can rewrite its own code to commit to any threat instantly. This reading explores why that changes the dynamics of negotiation entirely.

---

### 9. More advantages for digital agents

**Option A:**
Digital minds wouldn't just be faster thinkers — they could be copied, networked, expanded with new hardware, and run for centuries without forgetting anything. This reading lists the structural advantages that artificial minds would have over biological ones, many of which have no equivalent in nature.

**Option B:**
Speed is the obvious advantage, but it's only the beginning. Digital agents could share memories, coordinate perfectly with copies of themselves, swap in specialized modules, and never lose information. This excerpt catalogues the ways artificial minds could differ from biological ones — not just in degree, but in kind.

---

### 10. Security Mindset and Ordinary Paranoia

**Option A:**
Standard engineering asks "does this work?" The security mindset asks "how does this fail when something is actively trying to break it?" This article argues that building safe AI requires the same thinking used in cryptography — not piling up defenses, but designing systems where safety doesn't depend on being cleverer than the thing you're trying to contain.

**Option B:**
There's a difference between imagining what could go wrong and designing so that whole categories of failure can't happen. This article introduces the "security mindset" — a way of thinking borrowed from cryptography — and explains why it matters more for AI than for any system we've built before.
